Troubadors

Troubadours, is a novel about the lives of Latino musicians who play salsa in the clubs of Austin, Texas. Guillaume, the main character, has a problem. He is a balladeer with a peculiar incapacity. While he loves whole-heartedly, he knows not how to “unlove.” Haunted by the phantoms of lovers and friends who have either left him or died, he is imprisoned by the past: betrayal by the gypsy Marí, girlfriend of a ruthless drug lord, the untimely death of his mentor and his “Orpheus” Fernando, and the murder of Alba Rosa the Oaxacana, his one great love. To Guillaume, all love stories are ghost stories in the end.

In contrast, his good friend Vidal, the band’s conguero, lives in an unending present. He is a robust Falstaffian, a true “vato loco.” Vidal laughs at Guillaume’s melancholy pining. “Que maricón” he chides and offers practical advice on love, using his own near-sleazy amorous “aventuras” as cases in point.

When Guillaume learns of Alba Rosa’s mysterious death at the hands of a drug cartel in Oaxaca, he journeys through Mexico in order to uncover the circumstances of her murder. He meets Heliodoro, a Zapotec curandero who becomes his teacher. What Guillaume learns about the rhythms of a good life – and a good death – from the curandero in the mist-enshrouded Sierra, is revelatory.

Biography:

I am a musician and an English professor in Austin. The novel draws on both my experiences playing salsa in The Live Music Capital of the World, and my sojourns in Oaxaca. Troubadours is, in many respects, a love song to Austin and the Hill Country.
